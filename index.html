the whole argument in one line

From "what's the job?" to "why this tool?"

Here's how it's laid out. It begins broad (any model that fits parameters to data), pins down what "good" even means (a loss function), and only then brings in deep learning as one particular way to do that job — closing with the three reasons this particular way took over.

step 1 · the frame

A model captures structure and treats the rest as noise

A parametric model takes a set of parameters (called θ) and uses them to describe the meaningful pattern in your data. The twist: you decide what counts as structure by choosing the model. Anything the model can't represent is, by definition, noise. The whole game is then "pick a good model, find good parameters for it."

step 1b · two ways to be wrong

Approximation error vs. estimation error

Once you've picked a model and tried to fit it, the gap between your result and the truth splits into two distinct failures. Knowing which one you're suffering from tells you what to fix: a richer model, or better-estimated parameters.

step 2 · defining "good"

A loss function turns a vague wish into a number to minimize

Saying what we want is surprisingly hard, so we encode it as a loss — small when the model does well, large when it doesn't. Training is then just a search: keep nudging θ until the average loss across the data is as small as possible. (Classic example: the zero-one loss for cat-vs-dog — score 1 when wrong, 0 when right.)

step 3 · the tool, finally

Deep learning = many simple functions composed into one big function

A deep net is just lots of parameterized functions chained together. The prototype is the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP): each layer does a linear transform (Wx + b) followed by a nonlinearity (max(0, ·), i.e. ReLU), and you stack them. Numbers in, numbers out — but the stacking is where the power hides.

step 4a · the depth payoff

Adding depth multiplies expressiveness

Why this shape of function? Because depth buys a lot. A wide-enough net can approximate smooth functions (the Universal Approximation Theorem), but the striking result is that adding layers linearly can increase the number of distinct regions the network carves up exponentially — each new layer can fold the input space onto itself, roughly doubling the pieces.

step 4b · why this tool won

Three properties, all true at once

Plenty of function families could model the world. Deep nets took over because they hit all three of these simultaneously — and that combination is rare.

01 · flexible

Expressiveness

A small parameter budget can represent a huge variety of functions — and depth compounds it exponentially (the folding picture above).

02 · fast

Efficiency

Both the forward output and its derivatives compute quickly (linear in parameter count), so gradient-based training with SGD is practical. Specialized hardware — the AlexNet moment — made it real.

03 · generalizes

Generalization

Despite all that expressiveness, nets do well on unseen data. Counterintuitive, and still not fully explained — many theories, none fully satisfying.

the one-sentence takeaway

Deep learning is just a flexible, efficiently-trainable, surprisingly-generalizable way to pick a function out of a huge family — to capture the structure in your data. Seeing it as "a means to the parametric-modeling end" is the frame the author wants you carrying into the Transformer and BERT posts.
